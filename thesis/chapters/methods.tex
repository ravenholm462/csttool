\chapter{Methods}

\section{Pipeline Overview}
\label{sec:methods_pipeline}

\texttt{csttool} implements a six-stage processing pipeline that uses raw diffusion-weighted MRI data to generate hemisphere-specific CST bundles with quantitative tract metrics. Two ideas guided pipeline design: full automation without manual ROI placement, and modularity that allows each stage to be executed independently. Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline_overview} illustrates the processing stages and their data flow.

The six stages are:

\begin{enumerate}
	
	\item \hyperref[sec:methods_dataimport]{\textbf{Data Import}} — Accepts DICOM or NIfTI input, performs series selection and DICOM-to-NIfTI conversion where necessary, extracts acquisition metadata from BIDS JSON sidecars, and validates gradient table integrity.
	
	\item \hyperref[sec:methods_preprocessing]{\textbf{Preprocessing}} — Applies noise reduction to the diffusion-weighted volumes, generates a brain mask via Otsu thresholding on the $b = 0$ images, and optionally applies Gibbs unringing and motion correction.
	
	\item \hyperref[sec:methods_diffusiontensorfitting]{\textbf{Diffusion Tensor Fitting}} — Fits the diffusion tensor model to the preprocessed data and derives scalar maps including fractional anisotropy (FA), mean diffusivity (MD), radial diffusivity (RD), and axial diffusivity (AD).
	
	\item \hyperref[sec:methods_wholebraintractography]{\textbf{Whole Brain Tractography}} — Estimates a direction field using the Constant Solid Angle (CSA) ODF model, then performs deterministic streamline tracking seeded from white matter voxels to generate a whole-brain tractogram.
	
	\item \hyperref[sec:methods_atlasbasedextraction]{\textbf{Atlas-Based Extraction}} — Registers the MNI152 template to the subject's native space, warps the Harvard-Oxford cortical and subcortical atlases using the resulting deformation field, defines brainstem and motor cortex ROIs from the warped labels, and filters the whole-brain tractogram to isolate bilateral CST bundles.
	
	\item \hyperref[sec:methods_metrics]{\textbf{Tract-Level Metrics}} — Computes morphological and microstructural statistics for each hemisphere, derives along-tract profiles by resampling streamlines to equidistant points, and calculates laterality indices to determine asymmetry.
	
\end{enumerate}

Each stage reads from and writes to a structured output directory, producing intermediary NIfTI volumes, tractogram files (.trk), and JSON reports that record all processing parameters and information. This architecture ensures that any stage can be executed individually or inspected independently without the need to re-run the entire pipeline. For routine use, the command \texttt{csttool run} chains all stages together sequentially, as seen in Fig.~\ref{fig:pipeline_overview}. To chain multiple runs together, \texttt{csttool batch} can be used to process multiple subject in sequence, with per-subject timeout handling and consolidated reporting.

The pipeline is implemented entirely in Python and depends on a small set of open-source libraries. Tractography, tensor fitting, and registration are provided by DIPY~\cite{garyfallidis_dipy_2014}. NIfTI handling uses nibabel~\cite{markiewicz_2026_18944560}, image resampling uses nilearn~\cite{Nilearn}, and morphological operations use SciPy~\cite{virtanen2020scipy}. Atlas data is managed through a two-tier system: the MNI152 T1 1\,mm template~\cite{fonov2011unbiased} is bundled directly with the package and is always available; the FMRIB58 FA template~\cite{smith2006tract} and the Harvard-Oxford cortical and subcortical parcellations~\cite{jenkinson2012fsl} are distributed separately under an FSL licence (non-commercial use) and must be downloaded once with \texttt{csttool fetch-data --accept-fsl-license}. Version information for all libraries and the tool itself is recorded in every output report to support reproducibility.

The following sections describe each stage in detail, beginning with data import and concluding with metric computation.

\subsection{DICOM Input}
\label{subsec:methods_dicom}

When a DICOM directory is provided, the pipeline first catalogues all series contained within it. A recursive scan descends up to three directory levels, identifying DICOM-containing subdirectories either by the presence of \texttt{.dcm} or \texttt{.dicom} files or by reading the DICOM preamble magic number (\texttt{DICM} at byte offset 128). Each candidate directory is then analysed using pydicom~\cite{darcy_mason_2024_13824606} to extract series-level metadata: \texttt{SeriesDescription}, \texttt{ImageType} (distinguishing \texttt{ORIGINAL} from \texttt{DERIVED}), detected b-values, estimated volume count, and in-plane phase-encoding direction.

From this metadata each series is classified into one of nine types: raw multi-directional DWI (\texttt{DIFFUSION\_RAW}), trace-weighted (\texttt{DIFFUSION\_TRACE}), ADC map, FA map, other derived diffusion image (\texttt{DIFFUSION\_OTHER} --- matches diffusion keywords but carries a \texttt{DERIVED} or \texttt{SECONDARY} image-type flag), T1/T2 structural, localizer, or unknown. A numerical suitability score is then computed. Raw, non-derived DWI series receive a base score of 50; the \texttt{ORIGINAL} image-type flag adds 10 points; having at least two distinct b-values adds a further 15 points; and anterior-to-posterior (AP) phase encoding adds 10.\footnote{Additional scoring adjustments are applied: an extra 5 points if three or more distinct b-values are present; 5 points if an explicit $b = 0$ is detected; 20 or 10 points for series with $\geq 100$ or $\geq 50$ files respectively; $-10$ for fewer than 20 files; and $-10$ for posterior-to-anterior (PA) phase encoding.} Conversely, a \texttt{DERIVED} or \texttt{SECONDARY} image type subtracts 30, and non-diffusion series are penalised by 100, making them ineligible. Any series with a net positive score is considered suitable for tractography, and the highest-scoring series is selected automatically. The user may override automatic selection by specifying a series index or Series Instance UID on the command line.

The selected series is converted to compressed NIfTI format using \texttt{dicom2nifti}~\cite{dicom2nifti}, which handles vendor-specific encoding including Siemens mosaic multi-band acquisitions and multi-frame Enhanced DICOM objects, and extracts the diffusion gradient table (\texttt{.bval}/\texttt{.bvec}) directly from the DICOM headers. When \texttt{dicom2nifti} fails, a fallback conversion via \texttt{dcm2niix}~\cite{li2016first} is attempted if that tool is available on the system path.

\subsection{NIfTI Input}
\label{subsec:methods_nifti}

When a pre-converted NIfTI directory is provided, the stage loads the compressed volume (\texttt{.nii.gz}) and the accompanying gradient files directly. Both the \texttt{.bval}/\texttt{.bvec} and the \texttt{.bvals}/\texttt{.bvecs} filename variants are accepted, accommodating data exported by different software packages.

\subsection{Gradient Table and BIDS Metadata}
\label{subsec:methods_gradienttable}

Regardless of input format, the b-values and b-vectors are used to construct a DIPY \texttt{GradientTable} object~\cite{garyfallidis_dipy_2014}, which encodes the full diffusion-encoding scheme and is passed to subsequent pipeline stages. Volumes with $b \leq 50\;\text{s/mm}^2$ are treated as $b = 0$ reference images, following the default threshold used by the DIPY library~\cite{garyfallidis_dipy_2014}.

BIDS is shorthand for brain-imaging data structure and it attempts to provide a set of guidelines for file and metaddata naming and organization, statistical modeling of neuroimaging data and applications created to be used with said data~\cite{gorgolewski2016brain, karakuzu2022qmri}. If a BIDS-compatible JSON sidecar is present alongside the NIfTI file, the pipeline reads it to extract acquisition metadata. Echo time is stored in seconds in the BIDS convention and is converted to milliseconds internally. Field strength is also extracted where present. These values are recorded in the per-subject JSON import report alongside the library versions used, providing a complete record of acquisition context for downstream reporting.

\subsection{Denoising}
\label{subsec:methods_denoising}

The first step of the preprocessing pipeline is to attempt and remove noise from given MRI images. \texttt{csttool} offers two denoising techniques which are available in DIPY: non-local means (NLMEANS)~\cite{coupe2008nlmeans, coupe2012nlmeans} and Patch2Self~\cite{fadnavis2011patch2self, fadnavis2024patch2self2}.

\paragraph{NLMEANS} A straightforward way to denoise an image is to take the mean value of a group of pixels surrounding the target pixel. This is local means filtering. A non-local means filter looks beyond the immediate neighborhood of the target, taking a mean of all the pixels in the image and weighting them by a similarity function.

Given an image with an area $\Omega$ and two points $p$ and $q$, the algorithm for NLMEANS is~\cite{buades2011non}:

\begin{equation}
	\mathrm{NL}u(p) = \frac{1}{C(p)} \int_{\Omega} f(d(B(p), B(q)))u(q)dq
\end{equation}
\label{eq:nlmeans}

where $d(B(p), B(q))$ is an Euclidean distance between image patches centered respectively at $p$ and $q$, $f$ is a decreasing function and $C(p)$ is the normalizing factor, given by:

\begin{equation}
	C(p) = \int_{\Omega} f(p,q)dq
\end{equation}
\label{eq:nlmeans_normalizingfactor}

The weighting function $f(p,q)$ is used to determine how similar the image is at point $p$ to the image at point $q$. Commonly, the Gaussian weighting function is used:

\begin{equation}
	f(p,q) = e^{-\frac{|B(q)-B(p)|^2}{h^2}}
\end{equation}
\label{eq:gaussian_weight}

where $h$ is the standard deviation, and $B(p), B(q)$ are the local mean values surrounding the image point values $p$ and $q$ respectively.

\paragraph{Patch2Self} Classical denoising algorithms such as NLMEANS need to make assumptions on the input signal, which makes them prone to bias. Instead of this, Patch2Self only uses the fact that the noise across different 3D volumes of the diffusion signal originates from random fluctuations in the acquired signal. Patch2Self removes noise by predicting each diffusion volume $V_j$ from other volumes $V$, assuming noise is random and uncorrelated across them.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{figs/Patch2Self_Framework.png}
	\caption{Working principle of the Patch2Self algorithm~\cite{fadnavis2011patch2self}}
	\label{fig:patch2self}
\end{figure}

As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:patch2self}, Patch2Self operates in two stages. In the first stage (A), one diffusion volume $V_j$ is held out as the target. Patches from the remaining $(n-1)$ volumes $V_{-j}$ are used to predict the center voxel of the corresponding patch in $V_j$. The target volume itself is not used as input, which prevents the model from learning noise.

In the second stage (B), the trained regressor is applied to the same input volumes $V_{-j}$ to obtain a denoised estimate $\hat{V}_j$ of the held-out volume. This procedure is repeated for all volumes. Since noise is uncorrelated across volumes, it cannot be predicted and is therefore suppressed, while consistent structural information is preserved.

A marked advantage of Patch2Self is the fact that it can be plugged into any position in the preprocessing pipeline, because the only assumption it makes is that the noise is uncorrelated across volumes, which holds regardless of what other preprocessing steps have been applied.

\subsection{Gibbs Unringing}
\label{subsec:methods_gibbs}
Gibbs artifacts occur as a consequence of using Fourier transforms to reconstruct MR signals into images. Because an image has to be recreated by sampling only a finite number of frequencies, the Fourier series has to be cut short during reconstruction. The error due to this truncation is evident in high-contrast interfaces of the image. The resulting artifact usually manifests as intensity oscillations adjacent to edges of different tissue types~\cite{veraart2016gibbs}.

\begin{equation}
	I_{\mathrm{recon}} = I_{\mathrm{true}} * \mathit{sinc}
\end{equation}
\label{eq:mri_reconstruction}

\texttt{csttool} uses DIPY's Gibbs removal module. The algorithm is based on an adapted version of a sub-voxel Gibbs suppression procedure~\cite{kellner2016gibbs, neto2018advanced}. The technique takes advantage of the fact that the strength of these oscillations depends on how the underlying sinc pattern is sampled on the discrete voxel grid. If the sampling occurs near extrema of the sinc function, ringing is pronounced, whereas sampling near its zero-crossings minimizes the artifact. If sampling location is adjusted locally so that it occurs near zero-crossings, the ringing amplitude will be minimized.

First, a set of sub-voxel shifted images is generated by applying phase ramps in Fourier space:

\begin{equation}
	I_s(x)~\mathrm{for~different}~s
\end{equation}

For each voxel \(x\), the optimal shift is determined by minimizing local intensity oscillations, measured using a total variation criterion:

\begin{equation}
	r(x) = \arg\min_s \sum_n \left| I_s(x+n) - I_s(x+n-1) \right|
\end{equation}

This selects the shift that yields the least oscillatory neighborhood.

Finally, the corrected image is obtained by interpolating the optimally shifted signal back onto the original grid:

\begin{equation}
	I_{\text{unring}}(x) = I_{r(x)}\!\left(x - \frac{r(x)}{2M}\right)
\end{equation}

\subsection{Motion Correction}
\label{subsec:methods_motioncorrection}

Due to subject motion during acquisition, volumes become misaligned. The most common way to solve this problem is by performing a registration on each acquired volume to a reference $b=0$~\cite{jenkinson2001global}. The idea is to use a sequence of affine transformations in increasingly finer resolutions. DIPY's \texttt{register\_dwi\_series} function implements this idea in the \texttt{motion\_correction} wrapper.

A stable reference $b_0$ is built first. Afterwards, the motion correction pipeline is implemented, consisting of four stages: center-of-mass alignment, translation, rigid transformation and affine transformation.

First, center-of-mass alignment provides a coarse initialization by aligning the centroids $c_{\text{ref}}$ and $c_{\text{mov}}$ of the reference and moving volumes:

\begin{equation}
	t_{\text{COM}} = c_{\text{ref}} - c_{\text{mov}}
\end{equation}
\label{eq:motioncorrection_com}

Next, a translation-only model is optimized, where $x \in \mathbb{R}^3$ denotes a voxel coordinate and $t \in \mathbb{R}^3$ a translation vector:

\begin{equation}
	T(x) = x + t
\end{equation}
\label{eq:motioncorrection_translation}

This is followed by a rigid transformation, introducing a rotation matrix $R \in SO(3)$:

\begin{equation}
	T(x) = R x + t, \quad R \in SO(3)
\end{equation}
\label{eq:motioncorrection_rigid}

Finally, a full affine transformation is estimated with $A \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 3}$ capturing scaling and shear:

\begin{equation}
	T(x) = A x + t
\end{equation}
\label{eq:motioncorrection_affine}

At each stage, the transformation $T$ mapping the moving image $I_{\text{mov}}$ to the reference image $I_{\text{ref}}$ is obtained by minimizing a similarity cost function $C(\cdot)$:

\begin{equation}
	T^* = \arg\min_T C(I_{\text{ref}}, I_{\text{mov}} \circ T)
\end{equation}
\label{eq:motioncorrection_cost}

Here, $\circ$ denotes composition, i.e. resampling of $I_{\text{mov}}$ under the transformation $T$. The optimization is performed in a multi-resolution scheme, starting on coarse images and refining on finer scales to improve convergence.

It should be noted that this motion correction procedure addresses only rigid-body and affine misalignment caused by subject motion. It does not correct for eddy-current-induced geometric distortions or susceptibility-related field inhomogeneities. Eddy current correction typically requires reversed phase-encode acquisitions or specialised tools such as FSL's \texttt{eddy}~\cite{andersson2016integrated}, which depend on metadata (e.g.\ \texttt{TotalReadoutTime}, \texttt{PhaseEncodingDirection}) that may be absent in routine clinical protocols. Since \texttt{csttool} is designed to operate on single-shell clinical data with minimal acquisition requirements, eddy current correction is not included but is flagged as a limitation in Section~\ref{ch:discussion}.

\subsection{Reslicing}
\label{subsec:methods_reslicing}

At times, MR images need to be resliced in different resolutions. This is especially needed if datasets come with non-isotropic voxel sizes, which introduce directional bias in tractography: the fixed step size $h$ traverses more voxels along the short axis than the long axis, causing inconsistent spatial sampling and potentially distorting streamline geometry. Reslicing to isotropic voxels ensures uniform step-size behaviour across all orientations. \texttt{csttool} uses DIPY's \texttt{reslice} method. First, the function computes the scaling factor

\begin{equation}
	R = \frac{\text{new\_zooms}}{\text{zooms}}
\end{equation}

which defines the relative change in voxel size along each spatial dimension. If no target shape is provided, the new image dimensions are determined as

\begin{equation}
	\text{new\_shape} = \operatorname{round}\left(\frac{\text{zooms}}{\text{new\_zooms}} \cdot \text{shape}\right)
\end{equation}

so that the physical field of view is preserved while the voxel size changes. The image is then resampled onto the new grid using \texttt{affine\_transform} with \(R\) as the transformation matrix. For 4D datasets, this operation is performed independently for each volume.

Finally, the affine transformation is updated to remain consistent with the new voxel grid. A diagonal scaling matrix is constructed from \(R\),

\begin{equation}
	R_x =
	\begin{bmatrix}
		R_1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\
		0 & R_2 & 0 & 0 \\
		0 & 0 & R_3 & 0 \\
		0 & 0 & 0 & 1
	\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

and applied to the original affine as

\begin{equation}
	\text{affine}_{\text{new}} = \text{affine} \cdot R_x
\end{equation}

resulting in a resampled image with updated voxel spacing and consistent spatial coordinates.

\subsection{Brain Masking}
\label{subsec:methods_brainmasking}

For skull stripping, \texttt{csttool} uses DIPY's \texttt{median\_otsu} algorithm, which combines median filtering with Otsu thresholding to generate a binary brain mask.

Given a diffusion dataset $V \in \mathbb{R}^{X \times Y \times Z \times N}$, a reference image is first computed by averaging selected b$_0$ volumes with indices $\mathcal{I}$:

\begin{equation}
	I(x) = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{I}|} \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}} V(x,i)
	\label{eq:median_otsu_b0}
\end{equation}

To reduce noise, a median filter is applied iteratively. For a neighborhood $\mathcal{N}_r(x)$ with radius $r$, one filtering step is defined as:

\begin{equation}
	I^{(k+1)}(x) = \mathrm{median} \{ I^{(k)}(y) \mid y \in \mathcal{N}_r(x) \}
	\label{eq:median_otsu_median}
\end{equation}

and repeated for $K$ passes, resulting in a smoothed image $I^{(K)}$.

A global threshold $t^*$ is then computed using Otsu's method by maximizing the between-class variance:

\begin{equation}
	t^* = \arg\max_{t} \ \sigma_B^2(t)
	\label{eq:median_otsu_otsu}
\end{equation}

The binary mask $M$ is obtained as
\begin{equation}
	M(x) =
	\begin{cases}
		1, & I^{(K)}(x) > t^* \\
		0, & \text{otherwise}
	\end{cases}
	\label{eq:median_otsu_mask}
\end{equation}

Optional post-processing steps include binary dilation and removal of small holes or isolated regions. The final mask is applied to the input data:

\begin{equation}
	V_{\text{masked}}(x,i) = M(x) \cdot V(x,i)
	\label{eq:median_otsu_apply}
\end{equation}

Median filtering improves robustness to noise, while Otsu thresholding separates brain tissue from background based on the intensity histogram~\cite{yang2012improved}. However, Otsu's method assumes a bimodal intensity distribution, which may not hold under severe pathology or low SNR conditions. In such cases the mask can under-segment cortical regions or include non-brain tissue, propagating errors into subsequent tractography and metric stages.

\section{Diffusion Tensor Fitting}
\label{sec:methods_diffusiontensorfitting}

Fitting the diffusion tensor model is handled by the \texttt{fit\_tensors} method within the \texttt{track} module. For each voxel of the preprocessed volumes, this method estimates the diffusion tensor $\mathcal{D}$. This section describes how \texttt{cstttool} accomplishes this. The theoretical foundations are explained in Section~\ref{subsec:background_diffusiontensor}. The derived scalar metrics are defined in Section~\ref{subsec:background_dti}.

\paragraph{Tensor model and fitting.}

Eq.~\ref{eq:signal} models the diffusion-weighted signal using a scalar diffusion coefficient $D$. In the case of anisotropic diffusion, this parameter is replaced by the full diffusion tensor $\mathcal{D}$, yielding the tensor signal model~\cite{basser1994mr}:

\begin{equation}
	S_i = S_0 \exp~\!\bigl(-b\,\hat{\mathbf{g}}_i^\top \mathcal{D}\,\hat{\mathbf{g}}_i\bigr)
	\label{eq:tensor_signal}
\end{equation}

where $S_i$ is the signal measured along the $i$-th gradient direction with unit vector $\hat{\mathbf{g}}_i$, $S_0$ is the initial, unweighted signal and $b$ is the diffusion weighting factor (Eq.~\ref{eq:stejskal-tanner}). Since $\mathcal{D}$ is symmetric, it has six unique elements. Each gradient direction contributes exactly one equation of the form defined by Eq.~\ref{eq:tensor_signal}. If more than 6 non-colinear gradient directions are given, the system is overdetermined. As such, it can be solved via the ordinary least squares technique, which is the method applied by DIPY's \texttt{TensorModel} and used by \texttt{csttool}. Fitting is restricted to voxels within the brain mask obtained during preprocessing (Section~\ref{subsec:methods_brainmasking}).

\paragraph{Scalar map computation.} 

Eigenvalue decomposition of the fitted tensor (Eq.~\ref{eq:diffusion_matrix_form}) yields the eigenvalues $\lambda_1 \geq \lambda_2 \geq \lambda_3$, from which the four scalar maps FA, MD, AD and RD are computed as defined in Eqs.~\ref{eq:FA},~\ref{eq:MD},~\ref{eq:AD} and~\ref{eq:RD}. The implementation uses DIPY's \texttt{tenfit.fa} attribute for FA and the helper functions \texttt{mean\_diffusivity}, \texttt{radial\_diffusivity} and \texttt{axial\_diffusivity} from \texttt{dipy.reconst.dti} for MD, RD and AD respectively.

Noise can cause near-zero or negative eigenvalues to appear. This results in some voxels producing NaN or $\pm\infty$ values in derived scalar maps. These are replaced with zero, preventing numerical instability down the pipeline at the cost of underestimating diffusivity in affected voxels.

	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{figs/methods/tensor_maps.png}
	\caption{Scalar maps derived from the fitted diffusion tensor. Rows show axial, coronal, and sagittal views; columns display FA, MD, and the colour-coded principal eigenvector direction map (RGB). White matter structures exhibit high FA (bright in FA map) and directional coherence (consistent colours in the RGB map).}
	\label{fig:tensor_maps}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{White matter mask generation.} 

The end goal of \texttt{csttool} is to classify tracts belonging to the CST. As such, the detected streamlines must be contained to white matter only. If a co-registered T1-weighted anatomical image is not provided, the FA map provides a suitable proxy for white matter mask generation. This makes the tool more self-contained. A binary white matter mask $W$ is defined as:

\begin{equation}
	W(x) =
	\begin{cases}
		1, & \mathrm{FA}(x) > \tau \;\wedge\; M(x) = 1 \\
		0, & \text{otherwise}
	\end{cases}
	\label{eq:wm_mask}
\end{equation}

where $\tau$ is a configurable FA threshold (default $\tau = 0.2$) and $M$ is the brain mask from Section~\ref{subsec:methods_brainmasking}. In severely degenerated white matter, as may occur in advanced ALS, FA can drop below $\tau = 0.2$, potentially excluding pathologically relevant tissue from the mask and truncating streamlines prematurely. This limitation is inherent to FA-based masking and could be mitigated in future work by incorporating T1-weighted tissue segmentation when available. The mask is subsequently dilated by one voxel using \texttt{binary\_dilation} from SciPy to ensure that streamlines can reach the cortical grey--white matter interface, which is relevant for both seeding and termination during tractography.

\subsection{Direction Field Estimation}
\label{subsec:methods_directionfield}

The dominant streamline orientation within a given voxel is indicated by the primary eigenvector of the diffusion tensor. However, depending on the underlying tract architecture, the primary eigenvector may not actually align with any true fiber population. The Constant Solid Angle Orientation Distribution Function (CSA-ODF) is a more robust approach due to modeling the full orientation distribution within each voxel~\cite{aganj2010reconstruction}.

The choice of CSA-ODF over alternative models is driven by the single-shell constraint of routine clinical acquisitions. Constrained Spherical Deconvolution (CSD), while offering sharper angular resolution, requires multi-shell data to reliably estimate the fiber response function and separate tissue compartments~\cite{tournier2007robust}. Since \texttt{csttool} targets datasets acquired with a single non-zero b-value, CSD is not applicable. Compared to tracking directly on the DTI principal eigenvector $\mathbf{v}_1$, the CSA-ODF provides improved angular resolution in voxels where multiple fiber populations are present, particularly in the corona radiata where CST fibers cross with corpus callosum projections. The DTI eigenvector in such voxels points toward the average of all fiber populations rather than any individual tract, which can misdirect streamlines. The CSA-ODF resolves this ambiguity by reconstructing the full angular profile and selecting its dominant peak.

As described in Section~\ref{subsec:background_tractography}, the CSA-ODF is computed from single-shell diffusion-weighted data using the closed-form expression given by Eq.~\ref{eq:csa_odf}. In practice, the ODF is represented using a spherical harmonic (SH) basis as defined in Eq.~\ref{eq:sh_expansion}. The number of SH coefficients $R$ required for order $l$ is:

\begin{equation}
	R = \frac{(l+1)(l+2)}{2}
	\label{eq:sh_coeff_count}
\end{equation}

For a stable measurement, at least $R$ diffusion-weighted measurements are necessary. The default SH order is $l=6$ ($R=28$ coefficients). However, \texttt{csttool} will automatically reduce the order if the number of gradient directions is insufficient: order 8 requires $\geq 45$ directions, order 6 requires $\geq 28$, and order 4 requires $\geq 15$. This step ensures numerical stability across datasets that have varying angular resolutions.

The ODF is sampled on a symmetric sphere with 362 vertices (\texttt{symmetric362}). This sphere model was chosen to strike a balance between angular resolution and computational efficiency. From the reconstructed ODF, dominant fiber directions are extracted by identifying local maxima. For deterministic tracking, a single peak per voxel is selected according to:

\begin{equation}
	\mathbf{d}_{\text{peak}}(x) = \arg\max_{\hat{u} \in S^2} \text{ODF}(x, \hat{u})
	\label{eq:peak_detection}
\end{equation}

Two constraints are defined: first, the peak must exceed $80\%$ of the maximum ODF value (\texttt{relative\_peak\_threshold} = 0.8), and second, peaks must be separated by at least $45^{\circ}$ (\texttt{min\_separation\_angle} = 45) in case of multiple peaks. These parameters ensure that peaks arising from noise are filtered out.

Direction field estimation is implemented using DIPY's \texttt{CsaOdfModel} from \texttt{dipy.reconst.\-shm} and \texttt{peaks\_from\_model} from \texttt{dipy.direction}~\cite{garyfallidis_dipy_2014}. The sphere is loaded using \texttt{get\_sphere('symmetric362')} from \texttt{dipy.data}. Computation is restricted to voxels within the white matter mask $W$ from Eq.~\ref{eq:wm_mask} to reduce computational load and avoid estimating directions in non-white matter tissue.

\subsection{Seeding Strategy}
\label{subsec:methods_seeding}

By default, \texttt{csttool} generates an initial set of streamlines that sample all white matter pathways, instead of seeding only from specific ROIs. There are two reasons for this approach: first, this allows flexible downstream filtering, ensuring that no anatomically relevant tracts are missed due to restrictive seeding, and second, this allows \texttt{csttool} to be outfitted to potentially look for different structures in the brain, ensuring modularity.

Seeds are placed in all voxels where the FA exceeds a white matter threshold. The seed mask $M_{\text{seed}}$ is defined as:

\begin{equation}
	M_{\text{seed}}(x) =
	\begin{cases}
		1, & \mathrm{FA}(x) \geq \tau_{\text{seed}} \wedge W(x) = 1 \\
		0, & \text{otherwise}
	\end{cases}
	\label{eq:seed_mask}
\end{equation}

where $\tau_{\text{seed}} = 0.2$ is the default FA threshold and $W$ is the white matter mask from Eq.~\ref{eq:wm_mask}. The threshold of 0.2 is widely accepted as a conservative lower bound for white matter in healthy adult brain tissue~\cite{garyfallidis_dipy_2014}. Using the same threshold for both the white matter mask and seeding ensures consistency across the pipeline.

The total number of seeds is determined by the seed density $\rho$, which controls how many seeds are placed per qualifying voxel:

\begin{equation}
	N_{\text{seeds}} = \rho \cdot \sum_{x} M_{\text{seed}}(x)
	\label{eq:seed_count}
\end{equation}

The default seed density is $\rho = 1$ seed per voxel. Higher densities increase tractogram completeness at the cost of computational time and memory.

Seeds are generated in world coordinates (RASMM space) using DIPY's \texttt{seeds\_from\_mask} function from \texttt{dipy.tracking.utils}. World-space seeding ensures that streamline tracking is affine-invariant and that all spatial transformations are handled consistently via the affine matrix embedded in the NIfTI header.

\subsection{Streamline Propagation}
\label{subsec:methods_propagation}

Streamlines are propagated deterministically from each seed point by iteratively following the local fiber direction. For each position $\mathbf{x}_t$ along a streamline trajectory, the next step is computed as:

\begin{equation}
	\mathbf{x}_{t+1} = \mathbf{x}_t + h \cdot \mathbf{d}_{\text{peak}}(\mathbf{x}_t)
	\label{eq:propagation_step}
\end{equation}

where $h = 0.5$~mm is the step size and $\mathbf{d}_{\text{peak}}$ is the dominant ODF peak direction from Section~\ref{subsec:methods_directionfield}. This formulation is analogous to Eq.~\ref{eq:det_step}, but substitutes the CSA-ODF peak for the DTI principal eigenvector, yielding more accurate trajectories in regions with complex fiber architecture.

A default sub-voxel step size of $0.5$mm is chosen as the default to ensure that the algorithm can capture intra-voxel curvature and smoothly traverse voxel boundaries without overshooting true fiber geometry.

To ensure the streamline trajectory is not biased by the initial seed location along the tract, the propagation is performed bidirectionally: one streamline extends in the direction of the local peak, while another extends in the opposite direction. The two half-streamlines are concatenated to form a complete trajectory passing through the seed point.

Tracking is implemented using DIPY's \texttt{LocalTracking} class from \texttt{dipy.tracking.local\_tracking}~\cite{garyfallidis_dipy_2014}, which uses an explicit Euler integration scheme. A fixed random seed (\texttt{random\_seed} = 42) is used by default to ensure bitwise-identical tractograms across multiple runs with the same input data. 

\subsection{Stopping Criteria}
\label{subsec:methods_stopping}

Streamline propagation terminates when the trajectory enters tissue with low anisotropy or exits the brain volume. The primary stopping criterion is based on the FA threshold:

\begin{equation}
	\text{terminate if: } \mathrm{FA}(\mathbf{x}_t) < \tau_{\text{stop}}
	\label{eq:stopping_criterion}
\end{equation}

where $\tau_{\text{stop}} = 0.2$ by default, matching the seeding threshold $\tau_{\text{seed}}$. This symmetry ensures that streamlines begin and end in anatomically plausible white matter regions. Propagation into grey matter, cerebrospinal fluid, or other low-anisotropy tissue is prevented.

Optionally, the brain mask $M_{\text{brain}}$ from Section~\ref{subsec:methods_brainmasking} can be incorporated as a hard boundary constraint:

\begin{equation}
	\text{terminate if: } \mathrm{FA}(\mathbf{x}_t) < \tau_{\text{stop}} \vee M_{\text{brain}}(\mathbf{x}_t) = 0
	\label{eq:stopping_criterion_mask}
\end{equation}

This additional constraint prevents tracts from escaping the brain volume into background voxels.

The stopping criterion is implemented using DIPY's \texttt{ThresholdStoppingCriterion} from \texttt{dipy.tracking.stopping\_criterion}. When brain mask stopping is enabled, a modified FA map with zero values outside the mask is passed to the stopping criterion, effectively combining both conditions.

DIPY's \texttt{LocalTracking} also enforces an implicit angular constraint that terminates streamlines when the turning angle between consecutive steps exceeds a threshold (typically $60^{\circ}$). This constraint prevents anatomically implausible sharp turns and contributes to the overall quality of the tractogram by filtering trajectories that deviate abruptly from smooth fiber pathways.

\subsection{Choice of Deterministic Tracking}
\label{subsec:methods_deterministic_justification}

The CST is well-suited to deterministic tracking because it exhibits relatively low crossing-fiber complexity along most of its trajectory. The tract follows a coherent path from motor cortex through the internal capsule and cerebral peduncle to the brainstem. Crossing fibers are present near the cortical endpoints (where association and commissural fibers intersect) and in the corona radiata (where corpus callosum and superior longitudinal fasciculus fibers cross). In these regions, deterministic tracking may fail to resolve the CST contribution when it is not the dominant fiber population, potentially under-representing lateral cortical projections. Nevertheless, the core CST bundle maintains a dominant orientation that the CSA-ODF model reliably captures with a single peak per voxel, and the atlas-based filtering in Section~\ref{sec:methods_atlasbasedextraction} mitigates spurious streamlines from competing fiber populations.

Deterministic tracking sacrifices sensitivity to minor fiber populations but avoids the variance introduced by stochastic sampling inherent in probabilistic methods. This trade-off is deliberate: for clinical pipelines where between-run reproducibility is essential, the elimination of sampling noise outweighs the loss of sensitivity to lateral branching. Deterministic tracking produces identical streamlines for identical input parameters and data, as discussed in Section~\ref{subsec:background_tractography}.

\texttt{csttool} therefore prioritizes reproducibility and interpretability over sensitivity to lateral branching of streamlines. 

\subsection{Notes}
\label{subsec:methods_tractography_notes}

The whole-brain tractogram is written to disk in TrackVis \texttt{.trk} format, a widely supported binary format that stores streamline coordinates and the affine transformation matrix. The embedded affine ensures that streamlines can be correctly visualized and processed by third-party software without requiring separate metadata files.

This stage proceeds in four steps: image registration, atlas warping, ROI definition, and streamline filtering. \subsection{Image Registration}
\label{subsec:methods_imageregistration}

To warp atlas labels into subject space, a spatial mapping between the MNI152 standard-space coordinate system and the subject's native diffusion space is necessary. This transformation is computed in two stages: first, an affine registration, and second, a diffeomorphic refinement. The subject's FA map is chosen as the fixed (target) image throughout.

\paragraph{Template selection.}

The moving (source) image is preferentially the FMRIB58 FA template~\cite{smith2006tract}, a population-averaged fractional anisotropy map at 1\,mm isotropic resolution in MNI space. Because the subject's FA map is also an FA image, this yields a mono-modal registration in which both images share the same contrast, improving alignment quality. If the FMRIB58 template has not been installed (it requires a separate download under the FSL licence via \texttt{csttool fetch-data}), the pipeline falls back to the bundled MNI152 T1 1\,mm template~\cite{fonov2011unbiased}. In either case, the template is resampled to a common MNI grid before registration to ensure consistent voxel dimensions.

\paragraph{Affine registration.}

The affine stage estimates a global linear transformation from template space to subject space. As described in Section~\ref{subsubsec:background_registration}, the transformation takes the general form of Eq.~\ref{eq:affine_transform} and is optimized through a sequence of nested models of increasing complexity:

\begin{enumerate}
	\item \textbf{Centre-of-mass alignment} --- aligns the image centroids to provide a coarse initialisation.
	\item \textbf{Translation} --- optimises three translational parameters (3 DOF).
	\item \textbf{Rigid body} --- adds three rotational parameters (6 DOF).
	\item \textbf{Full affine} --- adds scaling and shearing (12 DOF).
\end{enumerate}

Each stage uses the result of the previous stage as its initialization, analogous to the motion correction pipeline described in Section~\ref{subsec:methods_motioncorrection}. The similarity metric used for optimization at each stage is Eq.~\ref{eq:mi} i.e. mutual information, estimated with 32 histogram bins. A three-level Gaussian pyramid controls the multi-resolution schedule: the default iteration counts are $[10\,000,\; 1\,000,\; 100]$ at downsampling factors $[4,\; 2,\; 1]$ with Gaussian smoothing of $\sigma = [3.0,\; 1.0,\; 0.0]$\,mm respectively.

The affine stage is implemented using DIPY's \texttt{AffineRegistration} class from \texttt{dipy.align.\-imaffine} with \texttt{MutualInformationMetric}~\cite{garyfallidis_dipy_2014}. The three transform models are provided by \texttt{TranslationTransform3D}, \texttt{RigidTransform3D} and \texttt{AffineTransform3D} from \texttt{dipy.align.\-transforms}.


\paragraph{Diffeomorphic registration.}

Affine registration is not sufficient to capture local anatomical differences between the subject and the template. A second, non-linear stage handles the registration of fine details using the Symmetric Normalization (SyN) algorithm~\cite{avants_symmetric_2008}. SyN computes a smooth, invertible displacement field $\mathbf{u}$ such that the composite transformation becomes:

\begin{equation}
	T_{\text{total}}(\mathbf{x}) = \bigl(\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x})\bigr) \circ T_{\text{affine}}(\mathbf{x})
	\label{eq:composite_registration}
\end{equation}

where $T_{\text{affine}}$ is the affine pre-alignment from the previous stage and $\circ$ denotes composition. The displacement field is obtained by solving the variational problem defined in Eq.~\ref{eq:syn_energy}, using local cross-correlation (Eq.~\ref{eq:cc}) as the similarity metric with a neighbourhood radius of 4 voxels. Default iteration counts are $[10,\; 10,\; 5]$ across three pyramid levels.

The implementation uses DIPY's \texttt{SymmetricDiffeomorphicRegistration} from \texttt{dipy.align.imwarp} with a \texttt{CCMetric} from \texttt{dipy.align.metrics}~\cite{garyfallidis_dipy_2014}. The resulting \texttt{DiffeomorphicMap} object provides both forward (template-to-subject) and inverse (subject-to-template) mappings.

Registration accuracy is a principal source of uncertainty in atlas-based tract extraction. Misregistration at cortical boundaries can shift motor cortex ROIs by several millimetres, altering which streamlines are accepted or rejected during filtering. FA maps lack cortical grey-matter contrast, which limits registration precision near the cortical surface compared to T1-weighted images. Visual quality control (Fig.~\ref{fig:registration_qc}) is therefore recommended for every subject, and the morphological dilation applied to ROIs in Section~\ref{subsec:methods_roidefinition} provides a spatial tolerance that partially absorbs small registration errors.

	\caption{Registration quality control. Rows show axial, coronal, and sagittal views; columns display the subject FA map, the warped MNI template, and an overlay of both. Accurate alignment of anatomical landmarks confirms successful registration.}
	\label{fig:registration_qc}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Atlas Warping}
\label{subsec:methods_atlaswarping}

With the spatial mapping computed, the Harvard-Oxford cortical and subcortical atlases~\cite{jenkinson2012fsl} are warped from MNI space into the subject's native diffusion space. The cortical atlas contains 48 regions per hemisphere (96 total), and the subcortical atlas contains 21 regions including the brainstem. Both atlases are maximum-probability maps thresholded at 25\,\%, distributed at 1\,mm resolution.

\paragraph{Hemisphere label separation.}

The Harvard-Oxford atlas assigns identical integer labels to corresponding regions in left and right hemispheres. To distinguish them, the hemispheres are separated \emph{before} the non-linear deformation is applied. In MNI space, the anatomical midline lies at $x=0$ and is geometrically straight, making the hemisphere assignment straightforward. Right-hemisphere labels receive a constant offset of 100:

\begin{equation}
	L'(\mathbf{x}) =
	\begin{cases}
		L(\mathbf{x}) + 100, & \text{if } x_{\text{world}} \geq 0 \\[2pt]
		L(\mathbf{x}), & \text{otherwise}
	\end{cases}
	\label{eq:hemisphere_offset}
\end{equation}

where $x_{\text{world}}$ is computed from the atlas affine matrix. Performing this split in standard space is important: the SyN deformation field warps the anatomical midline, so splitting at $x = 0$ after warping would produce asymmetric and inaccurate hemisphere boundaries.

\paragraph{Grid resampling and deformation.}

The Harvard-Oxford atlases are distributed on a $182 \times 218 \times 182$ voxel grid, which is different to the registration template grid. Before applying the deformation, both the cortical and the subcortical atlases are resampled to the template grid using nearest-neighbor interpolation to preserve label integrity. The forward mapping $T_{\text{total}}$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:composite_registration}) is then applied:

\begin{equation}
	L_{\text{subject}}(\mathbf{x}) = L'_{\text{MNI}}\!\bigl(T_{\text{total}}^{-1}(\mathbf{x})\bigr)
	\label{eq:atlas_warp}
\end{equation}

Nearest-neighbour interpolation is enforced during warping, as linear or spline interpolation would produce fractional label values at region boundaries. The warped labels are rounded to the nearest integer and cast to 16-bit signed integers. This operation is performed via DIPY's \texttt{DiffeomorphicMap.transform} method with \texttt{interpolation='nearest'}~\cite{garyfallidis_dipy_2014}.

\subsection{ROI Definition}
\label{subsec:methods_roidefinition}

Three regions of interest are extracted from the now-warped atlases to define the anatomical boundaries of the CST as described in Section~\ref{sec:background_anatomicalbackground}: a brainstem ROI marking the inferior endpoint, and bilateral precentral gyrus ROIs marking the superior (cortical) endpoints.

Each ROI is obtained by selecting voxels carrying a specific label and applying morphological dilation to enlarge the region:

\begin{equation}
	M_{\text{brainstem}} = \delta^{2}(L_{\text{subcortical}} = 8)
	\label{eq:brainstem_mask}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
	\begin{aligned}
		M_{\text{motor,L}} &= \delta^{1}(L_{\text{cortical}} = 7) \\[2pt]
		M_{\text{motor,R}} &= \delta^{1}(L_{\text{cortical}} = 107)
	\end{aligned}
	\label{eq:motor_masks}
\end{equation}

where $\delta^{n}$ denotes $n$ iterations of binary dilation, label~7 is the left precentral gyrus in the Harvard-Oxford cortical atlas, and label~107 is its right-hemisphere counterpart after the offset applied in Eq.~\ref{eq:hemisphere_offset}. The brainstem label~8 comes from the subcortical atlas.

The brainstem ROI receives two dilation iterations because the brainstem is a compact structure and streamlines frequently terminate just outside its boundary; greater spatial tolerance is needed to capture these endpoints. The motor cortex ROIs receive only one iteration to preserve lateral specificity near the interhemispheric fissure. Dilation uses SciPy's \texttt{binary\_dilation} from \texttt{scipy.ndimage}~\cite{virtanen2020scipy}.

The label extraction and dilation are implemented in \texttt{csttool}'s \texttt{create\_cst\_roi\_masks} function, which calls \texttt{extract\_roi\_mask} to produce a binary mask for each label and \texttt{dilate\_mask} to expand it by the specified number of iterations.

\subsection{Streamline Filtering}
\label{subsec:methods_streamlinefiltering}

The whole-brain tractogram from Section~\ref{sec:methods_wholebraintractography}, typically containing $10^5$ to $10^6$ streamlines, is filtered to retain only those connecting the brainstem to the motor cortex. Two filtering strategies are implemented, corresponding to the approaches described in Section~\ref{subsubsec:background_filteringstrategies}. Both strategies first apply a length constraint:

\begin{equation}
	20\;\text{mm} \;\leq\; L(s) \;=\; \sum_{i=0}^{N-1} \|\mathbf{p}_{i+1} - \mathbf{p}_{i}\| \;\leq\; 200\;\text{mm}
	\label{eq:length_bounds}
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{p}_0, \ldots, \mathbf{p}_N$ are the ordered points of streamline $s$. The lower bound excludes short spurious fragments from noise, and the upper bound excludes anatomically implausible trajectories.

\paragraph{Endpoint filtering.}

The strict method requires that one endpoint of a streamline lies within the brainstem ROI and the other within a motor cortex ROI. Because streamlines generated by bidirectional tracking have arbitrary orientation, both orderings are checked:

\begin{equation}
	\begin{aligned}
		\text{accept } s \;\text{ if: } \quad
		& \bigl(M_{\text{brainstem}}(\mathbf{p}_0) \;\wedge\; M_{\text{motor}}(\mathbf{p}_N)\bigr) \\
		\vee\; & \bigl(M_{\text{motor}}(\mathbf{p}_0) \;\wedge\; M_{\text{brainstem}}(\mathbf{p}_N)\bigr)
	\end{aligned}
	\label{eq:endpoint_condition}
\end{equation}

where $M_{\text{motor}} = M_{\text{motor,L}} \cup M_{\text{motor,R}}$. Streamline endpoints are converted from world coordinates to voxel indices via the inverse affine matrix, and the corresponding mask voxel is tested. This method yields high anatomical specificity but may discard streamlines that genuinely belong to the CST yet terminate slightly outside an ROI boundary.

\paragraph{Passthrough filtering.}

The default, more permissive method requires that a streamline passes through both the brainstem and a motor cortex ROI at any point along its trajectory, without constraining the endpoints:

\begin{equation}
	\text{accept } s \;\text{ if: } \quad
	\bigl(\exists\, i : M_{\text{brainstem}}(\mathbf{p}_i)\bigr)
	\;\wedge\;
	\bigl(\exists\, j : M_{\text{motor}}(\mathbf{p}_j)\bigr)
	\label{eq:passthrough_condition}
\end{equation}

Every point along the streamline is tested against the respective mask. This approach captures a larger proportion of CST streamlines, particularly those extending beyond the predefined ROI boundaries, making it more suitable for clinical datasets where cortical boundaries may be imprecise. Figure~\ref{fig:filtering_strategies} illustrates the difference between the two filtering strategies.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{figs/roi_operations.png}
	\caption{Conceptual comparison of endpoint filtering (D, F) and passthrough filtering (B, E). Endpoint filtering requires streamline terminals to lie within the ROIs, while passthrough filtering accepts any streamline that traverses both regions regardless of where it terminates~\cite{wakana2007reproducibility}.}
	\label{fig:filtering_strategies}
\end{figure}

Two additional exclusion criteria are applied to passthrough-filtered streamlines to remove false positives. First, streamlines that contact both the left and the right motor cortex ROIs are rejected, as they likely represent commissural fibres. Second, streamlines that extend substantially into both hemispheres are rejected:

\begin{equation}
	\text{reject if: } \quad \min_i(x_i) < -\tau_{\text{mid}} \;\wedge\; \max_i(x_i) > \tau_{\text{mid}}
	\label{eq:midline_crossing}
\end{equation}

where $x_i$ is the left--right world coordinate of point $\mathbf{p}_i$ and $\tau_{\text{mid}} = 8$\,mm. A streamline is only excluded if it reaches at least $\tau_{\text{mid}}$ into \emph{both} hemispheres, indicating a gross midline crossing characteristic of commissural pathways such as the corpus callosum or anterior commissure. Minor medial excursions are tolerated to accommodate the natural convergence of the CST near the brainstem and small imprecisions in the registration.

\paragraph{Hemisphere assignment.}

Each accepted streamline is assigned to the left or right CST based on which motor cortex ROI it traverses or terminates in. This assignment directly produces two separate tractograms, one per hemisphere.

\paragraph{Implementation.}

Endpoint filtering is implemented in the function \texttt{extract\_bilateral\_cst}, which converts each streamline's first and last world-coordinate points to voxel indices and tests them against the brainstem and motor masks. Passthrough filtering is implemented in \texttt{extract\_cst\_passthrough}, which iterates over every point of each streamline to test mask intersection and applies the bilateral-contact and midline-crossing exclusions.

The extracted CST bundles are written to disk as separate TrackVis \texttt{.trk} files (left, right, and combined) alongside a JSON report recording the filtering method, parameter values, and per-hemisphere streamline counts. The filtered tractograms serve as input to the metrics stage described in Section~\ref{sec:methods_metrics}. Typical clinical datasets yield $10^3$ to $10^4$ streamlines per hemisphere after filtering.

First, each hemisphere is analyzed independently during unilateral analysis, followed by a bilateral analysis that compares the hemispheres to assess asymmetry. The DTI metrics used throughout this stage --- fractional anisotropy (FA), mean diffusivity (MD), radial diffusivity (RD) and axial diffusivity (AD) --- are defined in Section~\ref{subsec:background_dti}. \subsection{Unilateral Analysis}
\label{subsec:methods_unilateral}

\paragraph{Morphological metrics.}

Three properties are used to describe the macroscopic geometry of the extracted bundle. The \emph{streamline count} is simply the number of streamlines retained after filtering. \emph{Length statistics} (mean, standard deviation, minimum, maximum) are computed from the Euclidean arc length of each streamline using DIPY's \texttt{length} function from \texttt{dipy.tracking.streamline}~\cite{garyfallidis_dipy_2014}. The \emph{tract volume} estimates the physical extent of the bundle by converting all streamline points from world coordinates to voxel indices via the inverse affine matrix, counting the number of unique occupied voxels, and multiplying by the voxel volume:

\begin{equation}
	V_{\text{tract}} = N_{\text{unique}} \times \prod_{d=1}^{3} \Delta_d
	\label{eq:tract_volume}
\end{equation}

where $N_{\text{unique}}$ is the number of distinct voxels traversed by at least one streamline point, and $\Delta_d$ are the voxel dimensions in millimetres extracted from the columns of the affine matrix. The result is reported in mm$^3$.

\paragraph{Microstructural metrics.}

For each DTI scalar map $S \in \{\text{FA},\, \text{MD},\, \text{RD},\, \text{AD}\}$, values are sampled at every point along every streamline. Each world-coordinate point $\mathbf{p}$ is converted to a voxel index by applying the inverse affine and rounding to the nearest integer, equivalent to nearest-neighbour lookup. Points that fall outside the volume boundaries are discarded. The resulting set of samples is aggregated into global summary statistics: mean, standard deviation, median, minimum and maximum.

\paragraph{Along-tract profiles.}

To capture how microstructural properties vary along the course of the tract, the per-streamline scalar samples are normalised to a fixed number of equidistant positions. Each streamline's sampled values are resampled to $n = 20$ points: streamlines with more than $n$ samples are downsampled by uniform index selection, while those with fewer are upsampled via linear interpolation. Streamlines yielding fewer than five valid samples are excluded to avoid poorly resolved profiles. The tract profile is then obtained as the pointwise mean across all retained streamlines:

\begin{equation}
	\bar{S}(k) = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{F}|} \sum_{s \in \mathcal{F}} S_s(k), \qquad k = 1, \ldots, n
	\label{eq:tract_profile}
\end{equation}

where $\mathcal{F}$ is the set of streamlines with at least five valid samples and $S_s(k)$ is the resampled scalar value of streamline $s$ at position $k$. The resulting 20-point vector summarises the spatial distribution of each metric from the inferior (brainstem) to the superior (cortical) end of the tract, following the along-tract profiling methodology described by Sarica et al.~\cite{sarica_corticospinal_2017}.

	\caption{Along-tract profiles for FA, MD, RD, and AD, comparing left and right CST hemispheres. Each profile shows the metric value at 20 equidistant positions from brainstem (left) to motor cortex (right), with shaded regions indicating the standard deviation across streamlines.}
	\label{fig:stacked_profiles}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Localized metrics.}

The 20-point tract profile is further subdivided into three anatomical regions to enable region-specific analysis:

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Pontine} (points 1--7, approximately 0--35\,\% of tract length)
	\item \textbf{Posterior limb of the internal capsule} (PLIC; points 8--14, approximately 35--70\,\%)
	\item \textbf{Precentral} (points 15--20, approximately 70--100\,\%)
\end{itemize}

For each region and each scalar map, the mean value across the corresponding profile points is computed. These localized statistics allow the bilateral comparison to detect focal asymmetries --- for example, a reduction in pontine FA that would be masked in a whole-tract average.

\subsection{Bilateral Analysis}
\label{subsec:methods_bilateral}

\paragraph{Laterality index.}

The primary asymmetry measure is the laterality index:

\begin{equation}
	LI = \frac{L-R}{L+R}
	\label{eq:laterality_index}
\end{equation}

where $L$ and $R$ are the metric values for the left and right hemisphere, respectively. The index is bounded between $-1$ and $+1$: positive values indicate left-hemisphere dominance, negative values indicate right-hemisphere dominance, and values near zero indicate symmetry. The laterality index is clinically relevant for ALS because upper motor neuron degeneration frequently presents with asymmetric onset, and the degree of inter-hemispheric asymmetry in CST metrics has been shown to correlate with lateralised clinical signs and disease progression~\cite{sarica_corticospinal_2017}. A laterality index is computed for every morphological metric (tract volume, streamline count, mean length), every global microstructural metric (mean FA, MD, RD, AD), and every localized microstructural metric (e.g.\ pontine FA, PLIC MD), yielding a comprehensive asymmetry profile.

\paragraph{Interpretation thresholds.}

Each laterality index is classified into one of four descriptive categories based on its absolute value:

\begin{equation}
	\text{asymmetry} =
	\begin{cases}
		\text{symmetric},            & |\mathrm{LI}| < 0.05 \\[2pt]
		\text{mild asymmetry},       & 0.05 \leq |\mathrm{LI}| < 0.10 \\[2pt]
		\text{moderate asymmetry},   & 0.10 \leq |\mathrm{LI}| < 0.20 \\[2pt]
		\text{strong asymmetry},     & |\mathrm{LI}| \geq 0.20
	\end{cases}
	\label{eq:li_thresholds}
\end{equation}

These thresholds provide a standardised descriptive framework, and are not intended as clinical diagnostic criteria.

\paragraph{Supplementary measures.}

Two additional measures complement the laterality index. The absolute difference $|L - R|$ provides the raw magnitude of asymmetry in the original units of the metric (e.g.\ mm$^3$ for volume, unitless for FA). The percent difference, defined as $100 \times |L - R| \,/\, \overline{M}$ where $\overline{M} = (L + R) / 2$, expresses asymmetry as a proportion of the mean bilateral value, offering a more clinically intuitive scale.
