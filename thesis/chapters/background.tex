\chapter{Background}
\label{ch:background}

This chapter serves to describe the framework needed to understand \texttt{csttool}'s implementation and results. It introduces the pathology of ALS, the neuroanatomy of the CST, as well as the fundamentals of diffusion MRI and DTI. In addition, it reviews different approaches to computing white matter tractograms and atlas-driven streamline extraction. Together, these concepts define the scientific context for the development of \texttt{csttool} and clarify how diffusion-derived metrics can be used to assess microstructural changes of the CST associated with ALS. 

\section{Clinical \& Anatomical Background}
\label{sec:background_anatomicalbackground}

\subsection{ALS}
Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis is a neurodegenerative, fatal disease of the central nervous system (CNS). ALS presents itself as a combination of upper motor neuron and lower motor neuron dysfunction, which leads to progressive weakness of the voluntary skeletal muscles in control of movement, swallowing, speaking and respiratory function. There are multiple phenotypes of the disease, with the most common being bulbar-onset ALS affecting the muscles of the face, jaw, throat and tongue, as well as spinal-onset ALS affecting the muscles of the arms, the upper trunk (cervical) and legs (lumbar). From onset, the disease spreads contralaterally, rostrally and caudally. ALS can also cause cognitive and behavioral changes. Normal language and executive function such as fluency, inhibition and working memory are negatively affected. \cite{feldman_amyotrophic_2022}

Diagnosis of ALS remains primarily clinical and is based on the identification of upper and lower motor neuron signs combined with disease progression, since no single definitive biomarker currently exists. Degeneration of upper motor neurons leads to structural and microstructural alterations of the corticospinal tract, the principal descending motor pathway of the brain. These changes can be detected using diffusion based magnetic resonance imaging techniques, which makes the corticospinal tract a key target for quantitative imaging approaches that aim to provide objective markers of disease related neurodegeneration.

\subsection{CST}
\label{subsec:background_cst}

\begin{figure}[tbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{figs/cst.png}
    \caption{CST anatomy (blue) conveying motor signals from the cortex to the skeletal muscles \cite{roos_studies_2013}}
    \label{fig:cst}
\end{figure}

The corticospinal tract (CST) is the principal descending motor pathway responsible for voluntary movement. The majority of CST axons originate in the primary motor cortex and, to a lesser extent, the premotor and somatosensory cortices. From the cortical motor areas, the axons descend through the corona radiata and converge in the posterior limb of the internal capsule (PLIC), which represents a major anatomical bottleneck where many motor fibers pass in close proximity. From there, the fibers continue through the cerebral peduncles of the midbrain and descend into the brainstem.

Along this trajectory the CST fibers maintain a largely organized arrangement until they reach the caudal medulla. At the junction between the brainstem and the spinal cord, the majority of CST fibers cross the midline in the pyramidal decussation. This crossing causes the motor cortex of one hemisphere to control voluntary movements on the contralateral side of the body. After decussation, the fibers descend within the lateral columns of the spinal cord, where they terminate at different spinal levels by synapsing onto interneurons and lower motor neurons that ultimately innervate skeletal muscles. \cite{welniarz_corticospinal_2017}

Degeneration of upper motor neurons in ALS is closely associated with structural and microstructural damage of the corticospinal tract. Postmortem studies have shown that the CST columns undergo degeneration and sclerosis, reflecting the loss of axons and associated myelin. These pathological changes often occur in both hemispheres, although the degree of degeneration may differ between sides. This bilateral but potentially asymmetric involvement makes the CST a key target for diffusion MRI based studies that aim to quantify disease related changes in white matter integrity.

These microstructural changes cannot be directly visualized with conventional structural MRI, but they alter the diffusion behavior of water molecules within white matter, making diffusion-weighted MRI a suitable modality for detecting and quantifying degeneration of the corticospinal tract.

\section{Diffusion MRI Fundamentals}
\label{sec:background_dwifundamentals}

\subsection{Basics}
\label{subsec:background_dwibasics}

Diffusion-weighted imaging (DWI) is an MRI technique that generates image contrast based on the diffusion of water molecules. Diffusion, or Brownian motion, refers to the random thermal movement of particles. In a homogeneous medium, diffusion is isotropic—occurring equally in all directions. In biological tissue, however, cellular structures such as membranes and organelles hinder free motion, causing diffusion to become directionally dependent, or anisotropic. The degree and orientation of this anisotropy reflect tissue microstructure and can change in various pathological conditions, making DWI an essential tool in neuroimaging.

\subsection{Generation of contrast}
\label{subsec:background_dwicontrast}

MRI traditionally measures signals from hydrogen nuclei ($^1$H) of water and fat molecules. These nuclei possess an intrinsic property called spin, which enables magnetic resonance. To make the MRI signal sensitive to diffusion, pairs of diffusion gradients (DGs) are applied around a refocusing $180^{\circ}$ pulse in a spin-echo sequence. For stationary spins, the accumulated phase from the first DG is fully reversed by the second DG. On the other hand, diffusing spins will have moved to a different environment before the second DG can refocus them. Subsequently, they fall out of phase and the signal is lost. This creates contrast between stationary and diffusing spins. Fig.~\ref{fig:dwi_sequence} offers a graphical explanation of this process.

\subsection{\textit{b}-value}
\label{subsec:background_bvalue}

The degree to which diffusion affects the resulting image is called the $b$-value. It is an operator controlled parameter that affects how quickly the signal from a diffusion-weighted image decays:

\begin{equation}
    S(b) = S_0 \cdot \exp(-bD)
    \label{eq:signal}
\end{equation}

where $S_0$ is the signal without diffusion weighting ($b = 0$) and $D$ is the diffusion coefficient for a unit area per unit time. Consequently, the unit for the $b$-value is unit time per unit area.

In relation to the properties of applied rectangular diffusion gradients, the $b$-value is modeled as:

\begin{equation}
    b = \gamma^2 G^2 \delta^2 (\Delta - \delta/3)
    \label{eq:stejskal-tanner}
\end{equation}

where $\gamma$ is the gyromagnetic ratio in $\frac{\mathrm{rad}}{\mathrm{s \cdot c}}$, $G$ is the gradient magnitude in $[\frac{\mathrm{T}}{\mathrm{m}}]$, $\delta$ is the gradient duration in $[\mathrm{s}]$ and $\Delta$ is the time interval separating the diffusion gradient in in $[\mathrm{s}]$ \cite{stejskal_spin_1965}. A visual explanation is provided in Fig.~\ref{fig:stejskal_tanner}. The $b$-value depends on the strength, duration and spacing of diffusion gradients. In practice, the $b$-value is given in units $[\frac{\mathrm{s}}{\mathrm{mm^2}}]$. There is no singular optimal choice for $b$; it is chosen based on number of signals averaged, on features of the patient and on predicted pathology.

In practice, most routine clinical DWI studies use \textit{b}-values between $0$ and $1000$ $s/mm^2$, although higher values are also possible depending on the scanner. A value of $1000 s/mm^2$ is chosen as the clinical standard for certain pathologies such as brain tumors \cite{kazemzadeh_role_2025}.

\subsection{Diffusion tensor}
\label{subsec:background_diffusiontensor}

Eq.~\ref{eq:signal} uses the diffusion coefficient $D$ to model Brownian motion. This is an overly simplistic approach that assumes isotropic motion of spins and is inapplicable to heterogenous environments such as the intracellular space. The diffusion tensor $\mathcal{D}$ expands on the diffusion coefficient $D$ by taking into account both the rates of diffusion and their direction. Typically, it is represented as a $3 \times 3$ array:

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{D} = 
    \begin{bmatrix}
        D_{xx} & D_{xy} & D_{xz}\\
        D_{yx} & D_{yy} & D_{yz} \\
        D_{zx} & D_{zy} & D_{zz}
    \end{bmatrix} 
    \xrightarrow{\text{isotropic}}
    \begin{bmatrix}
        D & 0 & 0\\
        0 & D & 0 \\
        0 & 0 & D
    \end{bmatrix} 
\end{equation}

In the case of anisotropic diffusion, the most common approach of obtaining $\mathcal{D}$ is to estimate its components from a set of diffusion-weighted measurements acquired along multiple gradient directions. Using eigenvalue decomposition, $\mathcal{D}$ can be expressed in terms of its eigenvalues $\lambda_i$ and eigenvectors $\mathbf{v}_i = [v_{i,x}~v_{i,y}~v_{i,z}]$ as

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{D} \mathbf{v} = \lambda \mathbf{v}
\end{equation}

The eigenvectors in $\mathbf{v}$ represent the principal directions of diffusion, while the eigenvalue $\lambda$ represents its magnitude.

In matrix form, $\mathcal{D}$ can also be represented as

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{D} = \mathbf{V} \Lambda \mathbf{V}^T
    \label{eq:diffusion_matrix_form}
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{V} = [\mathbf{v}_1~\mathbf{v}_2~\mathbf{v}_3]$ and $\Lambda = \mathrm{diag}(\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \lambda_3)$, or

\begin{equation}
\Lambda = 
    \begin{bmatrix}
        \lambda_1 & 0 & 0\\
        0 & \lambda_2 & 0 \\
        0 & 0 & \lambda_3
    \end{bmatrix}
    \label{eq:biglambda}
\end{equation}

This formulation is useful because it geometrically maps to a diffusion ellipsoid oriented along $\mathbf{v}_i$ and scaled by $\sqrt{\lambda_i}$, as seen in Fig.~\ref{fig:diffusion_ellipsoid}. Under this model, for $\lambda_1 = \lambda_2 = \lambda_3 = \lambda$, the ellipsoid reduces to a sphere, representing isotropic diffusion.

\begin{figure}[hbt!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{figs/diffusion_ellipsoid.png}
    \caption{Diffusion ellipsoid with directions shown by eigenvectors $\mathbf{\epsilon}_i$ and magnitudes shown by eigenvalues $\lambda_i$. Courtesy of Allen D. Elster, MRIquestions.com}
    \label{fig:diffusion_ellipsoid}
\end{figure}

There are two benefits to using the diffusion ellipsoid approach. 

First, $\mathcal{D}$ can be determined from measurements obtained from any frame of reference, making quantification of diffusion rotation-invariant. 

Second, in the principal diffusion frame (Eq.~\ref{eq:diffusion_matrix_form}), the off-diagonal elements of $\mathcal{D}$ vanish thanks to Eq.~\ref{eq:biglambda}, reducing the tensor to a diagonal form and thereby simplifying computation and interpretation.

Although a minimum of $6$ gradient directions are required to fit a diffusion tensor, in practice $\geq 30$ directions are used, with $\geq 64$ being preferred. This is necessary to reduce errors due to crossing, kissing or branching fibers.

\subsection{DTI}
\label{subsec:background_dti}

Diffusion tensor imaging (DTI) uses the eigenvalues and eigenvectors obtained from $\mathcal{D}$ to quantify diffusion properties of a tissue. The most commonly used metrics are axial diffusivity AD, radial diffusivity RD, mean diffusivity MD and fractional anisotropy FA.

AD is given by Eq.~\ref{eq:AD} and describes the magnitude of apparent diffusion along the principal axis. It corresponds to the first eigenvalue of $\mathcal{D}$.

\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{AD} = \lambda_1
    \label{eq:AD}
\end{equation}

RD is given by Eq.~\ref{eq:RD} and represents the average apparent diffusion along the secondary and tertiary diffusion axes i.e. perpendicular to the principal axis.

\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{RD} = \frac{\lambda_2 + \lambda_3}{2}
    \label{eq:RD}
\end{equation}

MD is given by Eq.~\ref{eq:MD}, describing the average amount of diffusion in a given voxel. It is an absolute measure and is independent of direction. 

\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{MD} = \frac{\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \lambda_3}{3}
    \label{eq:MD}
\end{equation}

FA is given by Eq.~\ref{eq:FA} as a relative measure describing the amount of diffusion asymmetry in a voxel. FA values vary between $0$ and $1$. Low FA values generally indicate isotropic diffusion; high FA values generally indicate diffusion along only one direction.

\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{FA} = \sqrt{\frac{(\lambda_1 - \lambda_2)^2 + (\lambda_2 - \lambda_3)^2 + (\lambda_3 - \lambda_1)^2}{2(\lambda_1^2 + \lambda_2^2 + \lambda_3^2)}}
    \label{eq:FA}
\end{equation}

Although commonly used as biomarkers in diffusion MRI studies, the previously described DTI metrics face limitations when tackling voxels with a large amount of crossing white fiber tracts. Fig.~\ref{fig:dti_limits} demonstrates how changes in FA, or a lack thereof, do not necessarily correspond to true alterations in white matter microstructure.

However, these limitations do not speak against DTI as a valuable tool for neuroimaging. First, the issues described in Fig.~\ref{fig:dti_limits} arise primarily due to the nature of FA as a relative measure. Staying within the framework of DTI, using non-relative values such as MD or the trace of $\mathcal{D}$ (i.e. total apparent diffusion along the three tensor axes; $\mathrm{Trace} = \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \lambda_3$) offers more robust measurements of areas strongly riddled with fiber crossings. This is because they do not only account for, but also equally weight the amount of diffusion along all three axes of $\mathcal{D}$. Indeed, in Fig.~\ref{fig:dti_limits} both the MD and the trace would increase as expected, as diffusion in the voxel would be less restricted. Second, inaccuracies due to high density of fiber crossings are less likely to occur when imaging regions where complex fiber geometries are generally not expected, such as the CST or the corpus callosum. Finally, for clinical findings, the results of DTI should always be corroborated with other imaging metrics to ensure result validity. \cite{figley_potential_2022}

\subsection{White Matter Tractography}
\label{subsec:background_tractography}

The primary eigenvector of the diffusion tensor $\mathcal{D}$ can be used to obtain the three-dimensional representation of the white matter pathways or fiber bundles. The method of projecting 3D trajectories of fiber pathways between different brain systems \textit{in vivo} is called tractography. The resulting approximation allows for both visualization and quantitative analysis of specific tracts \cite{soares_hitchhikers_2013}.

The process of tractography generally has three stages: seeding, propagation and termination. Seeding is the act of defining starting points in the image from which the fiber tracts will be drawn. One approach is to generate the fibers only from specific regions. Another is to seed large portions of the brain, then subsequently selecting only bundles belonging to regions of interest.

Propagation determines the algorithm used to follow along the tract as it travels through the brain. Two main categories of propagation algorithms exist: deterministic and probabilistic. 

In deterministic tractography, a streamline is propagated by following the principal eigenvector $\mathbf{v}_1$ of the local diffusion tensor at each step. Given a current position $\mathbf{x}_t$, the next position is:

\begin{equation}
	\mathbf{x}_{t+1} = \mathbf{x}_t + h \cdot \mathbf{v}_1(\mathbf{x}_t)
	\label{eq:det_step}
\end{equation}

where $h$ is the step size. For a fixed diffusion dataset, given identical input parameters, multiple tractography runs will always produce the same result. This makes the algorithm reproducible.

Probabilistic tractography aims to model the uncertainty of local fiber direction by sampling from an orientation distribution function (ODF) at each step \cite{lazar_mapping_2010}. If the algorithm runs from the same seed repeatedly, a distribution of plausible pathways will be returned instead of a single trajectory. This approach is useful for areas with high volumes of crossing fibers or low FA.

\subsection{Atlas-Based Tract Extraction}
\label{subsec:background_atlasbasedextraction}

Whole-brain tractography results in a vast number of fibers, with the orders of magnitude typically being $10^6$ or $10^7$. To proceed with quantitative analysis, the relevant streamlines first need to be extracted from the full dataset according to anatomical criteria. The gold standard is manual placement of ROIs by an expert. The ROIs define inclusion or exclusion zones for the tracts of interest. While this method is very accurate, it has the following disadvantages: it is time-consuming, it requires an expertly trained individual, and the results are prone to inter-operator variability.

Atlas-based methods seek to address these limitations by automating ROI placement by transferring anatomical definitions from a reference atlas to individual subject space. First, anatomical knowledge about the tract trajectories, encoded in a standardized coordinate system, is warped to each subject's native diffusion space using image registration. Streamlines are then filtered according to their intersection with the warped ROIs. This approach eliminates the need for manual ROI placement while maintaining anatomical validity.

\subsubsection{Filtering Strategies}
\label{subsubsec:background_filteringstrategies}

Two principal strategies of filtering streamlines based on ROI intersection exist: endpoint and passthrough filtering. Endpoint filtering requires that the first and last coordinate of the streamline fall within the specified ROIs. This results in a strict requirement that ensures the selected streamlines genuinely connect to the regions of interest. Passthrough filtering is a more lenient method that retains streamlines that merely traverse the specified ROIs at any point along their trajectory. The end result is a more sensitive filtering approach at the cost of potentially including streamlines that are not wanted.

Both strategies can be combined with logical operations---AND (intersection), NOT (exclusion), and CUT (truncation)---to refine tract selection \cite{wakana2007reproducibility}. The AND operation retains only streamlines satisfying multiple ROI constraints, while NOT operations exclude streamlines passing through anatomically implausible regions. Fig.~\ref{fig:roi_operations} offers a graphical explanation.

\subsubsection{Image Registration}
\label{subsubsec:background_registration}

To ensure successful atlas-based extraction, accurate spatial correspondence between atlas and subject spaces is required. Two main registration strategies are commonly used.

\paragraph{Streamlines-to-atlas} The goal of this approach is to warp the subject's tractography to a standardized coordinate system. The first step is to apply an affine transformation to align global brain geometry. Second, local anatomical variations are accounted for by applying a non-linear transform. The resulting deformation is applied to every streamline point, after which atlas-defined ROIs in standard space can be used to filter streamlines. This approach is convenient for batch analyses because all subjects share a common coordinate frame. However, streamline-to-atlas methods are computationally demanding due to the large number of streamline coordinates that must be transformed. In addition, nonlinear deformation fields can alter streamline geometry, which may affect tract-based analyses.

\paragraph{Atlas-to-subject} The inverse approach keeps streamlines in native space while warping atlas ROIs to each subject. The subject's anatomical (e.g. T1-weighted) or diffusion-derived (e.g. FA) image is used to compute the registration. The resulting transformation is then applied to the atlas labels. This approach preserves the streamline geometry produced during tractography and maintains the spatial relationships present in the subject's diffusion space, which is advantageous when analyzing subject-specific anatomy or pathology.

\subparagraph{Affine Registration}

Affine registration estimates a linear spatial transformation mapping one image onto the other.

\begin{equation}
	\mathbf{x}' = A\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{t}
	\label{eq:affine_transform}
\end{equation}

$\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^3$ is a voxel coordinate, $A \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 3}$ encodes rotation, scaling and shearing, and $\mathbf{t} \in \mathbb{R}^3$ is a translation vector. In practice this transformation is usually estimated through a sequence of nested models of increasing complexity: translation (3 degrees of freedom, or DOF), rigid body (6 DOF; three rotations and three translations), and full affine (12 DOF). The result of each stage is used as initialization for the next.

The standard similarity metric for affine registration in multi-modal settings is mutual information (MI) \cite{maes2002multimodality, mattes2003pet}, defined as

\begin{equation}
	\mathrm{MI}(I, J) = H(I) + H(J) - H(I, J)
	\label{eq:mi}
\end{equation}

where $H(I)$ and $H(J)$ are the marginal Shannon entropies of the two images and $H(I,J)$ is their joint entropy. These quantities are estimated from the joint intensity probability distribution derived from the joint histogram of the images. MI measures how much information one image provides about the other, which makes it suitable even when the intensity relationships between modalities differ.

Optimization typically follows a multi-resolution strategy using a Gaussian pyramid. Images are progressively smoothed and sub-sampled, and optimization is performed at each level, with the resulting parameters used to initialize the next level. This coarse-to-fine procedure reduces the risk of convergence to local optima.

\subparagraph{Diffeomorphic Registration}

A diffeomorphism is a smooth and invertible mapping between two spaces that preserves their differentiable structure. Diffeomorphic transformations deform an image while preserving topology, meaning structures cannot fold over themselves and the mapping remains invertible everywhere. Such transformations are required because affine models cannot represent local anatomical differences between subjects.

A widely used approach in medical image registration is the symmetric normalization (SyN) algorithm \cite{avants_symmetric_2008}. SyN formulates registration as a symmetric variational problem in which the transformation $\varphi$ is decomposed into two components, $\varphi_1$ and $\varphi_2$, that evolve in opposite directions. This ensures that the registration result does not depend on which image is chosen as fixed or moving.

The diffeomorphic constraint is enforced by constructing the transformation from time-varying velocity fields $v_i$ integrated over time:

\begin{equation}
	\frac{d\varphi_i(\mathbf{x}, t)}{dt} = v_i(\varphi_i(\mathbf{x}, t), t),
	\quad \varphi_i(\mathbf{x}, 0) = \mathbf{Id}
	\label{eq:syn_ode}
\end{equation}

The energy functional minimized by SyN with cross-correlation (CC) as the similarity metric is \cite{avants_symmetric_2008}

\begin{equation}
	E_{cc}(\tilde{I},\tilde{J}) =
	\inf_{\varphi_1} \inf_{\varphi_2}
	\int_{t=0}^{0.5}
	\left\{
	\|v_1(\mathbf{x},t)\|_L^2 + \|v_2(\mathbf{x},t)\|_L^2
	\right\} dt
	+ \int_\Omega CC(\tilde{I}, \tilde{J}, \mathbf{x})\, d\Omega
	\label{eq:syn_energy}
\end{equation}

where $\|\cdot\|_L$ denotes the norm induced by the regularization operator $L = a\nabla^2 + b\mathbf{Id}$ (with constants $a, b$), which enforces spatial smoothness of the velocity field.

The local cross-correlation at position $\mathbf{x}$ is computed over a neighbourhood window of size $n^3$:

\begin{equation}
	CC(\tilde{I}, \tilde{J}, \mathbf{x})
	= \frac{\langle \tilde{I}, \tilde{J} \rangle^2}{\langle \tilde{I} \rangle\langle \tilde{J} \rangle}
	= \frac{A^2}{BC}
	\label{eq:cc}
\end{equation}

where $\tilde{I}$ and $\tilde{J}$ denote locally mean-subtracted image intensities \cite{avants_symmetric_2008}. The terms $A$, $B$, and $C$ represent the local covariance and variance components used to compute normalized cross-correlation. Because CC depends on local image statistics, it is robust to spatially varying intensity inhomogeneities commonly present in MRI data.
